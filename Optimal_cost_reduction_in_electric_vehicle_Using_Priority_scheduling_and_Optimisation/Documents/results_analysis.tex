\noindent This chapter details the work done and the results obtained from the proposed Two-Stage Priority-Guided Genetic Algorithm (GA) framework for EV charging/discharging scheduling. The framework is benchmarked against two classical baselines: First-Come-First-Serve (FCFS) and Shortest-Job-First (SJF), across increasing fleet sizes and under various sensitivity scenarios.

\section{Activities Completed}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Surveyed recent literature on EV charging scheduling, covering methods such as Linear Programming, Dynamic Programming, MILP, and Metaheuristics (GA, PSO, GSA). Identified key research gaps in holistic multi-objective optimization that jointly models cost, degradation, grid stability, and user satisfaction.
    \item Designed a Two-Stage scheduling framework:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Stage-I}: Priority-based charger admission using four normalized factors --- Price, Battery Degradation, Grid Stress, and User Urgency.
        \item \textbf{Stage-II}: Genetic Algorithm (GA) based power optimization for admitted EVs using DEAP, with SBX crossover, polynomial mutation, tournament selection, and constraint repair.
    \end{itemize}
    \item Implemented the full simulation pipeline (\texttt{pipeline.py}) supporting configurable EV fleet generation, time-of-use pricing, bidirectional charging (G2V/V2G), and multi-objective fitness evaluation.
    \item Conducted comparative experiments across six fleet sizes: $N \in \{50, 100, 150, 200, 250, 300\}$ EVs with $M = 10$ chargers, comparing Proposed (Priority+GA), FCFS, and SJF scheduling strategies.
    \item Performed sensitivity analyses on three parameters: (a) Charger Power Mix, (b) Fleet Composition, and (c) Initial SoC Distribution.
    \item Conducted GA convergence analysis across varying fleet sizes ($N = 20$ to $N = 100$).
    \item Built a web-based demonstration interface with interactive scheduling studio and real-time visualization of charging schedules.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Results}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\noindent The simulation models a workplace parking lot with $M = 10$ bidirectional charging points operating over a 12-hour horizon (8 AM--8 PM), discretized into $T = 48$ slots of 15 minutes each. EVs arrive stochastically with random initial SoC, battery capacity, and stay duration. The GA is configured with population size $= 120$, maximum generations $= 300$, crossover probability $p_c = 0.9$, mutation probability $p_m = 0.3$, SBX crossover index $\eta_c = 20$, polynomial mutation index $\eta_m = 20$, and tournament size $= 3$. Objective weights are set to $w_1 = w_2 = w_3 = w_4 = 0.25$ for equal contribution.

\subsection{Discussion}
% ============================================================

\noindent The experimental results collectively validate the effectiveness of the proposed Two-Stage Priority-Guided GA framework. The key observations are summarized below:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Cost Reduction}: The proposed method achieves \textbf{86--92\%} cost reduction compared to FCFS, by optimally scheduling V2G discharging during peak-price periods and G2V charging during off-peak hours.

    \item \textbf{Battery Health}: Degradation cost is reduced by \textbf{$\approx$87\%} compared to FCFS, as the GA avoids aggressive micro-cycling and distributes energy exchange over longer periods.

    \item \textbf{Grid Stability}: Grid load variance is reduced by \textbf{33--44\%} relative to FCFS, promoting a smoother aggregate load profile that reduces stress on distribution transformers.

    \item \textbf{Waiting Time}: For larger fleets ($N \geq 150$), the priority-based admission mechanism achieves lower waiting times than both baselines, owing to efficient charger reallocation when target SoC is met.

    \item \textbf{Fairness Trade-off}: The proposed method exhibits a higher Gini index than FCFS, indicating that priority scheduling intentionally serves urgent EVs first. This is a deliberate design decision --- system-level cost savings and grid stability are prioritized over strict equality.

    \item \textbf{Scalability}: The framework scales gracefully with fleet size. Cost per EV \textit{decreases} with larger fleets (from \$0.95 at $N=50$ to \$0.33 at $N=300$), demonstrating economies of scale in coordinated scheduling.

    \item \textbf{Robustness}: Sensitivity analyses confirm that the framework's advantages are preserved under varying charger power ratings, fleet compositions, and initial SoC distributions.

    \item \textbf{Convergence}: The GA reliably converges within 200 generations for all tested fleet sizes, with early stopping ensuring computational efficiency.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent Overall, the results demonstrate that the proposed framework effectively balances system-level objectives (cost, degradation, grid stability) with individual user requirements (satisfaction, waiting time), outperforming conventional FCFS and SJF scheduling strategies across all evaluated conditions.
